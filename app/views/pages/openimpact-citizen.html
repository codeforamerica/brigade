Open government is a global movement to bring government into the digital age by making public data open by default and creating new opportunities for civic engagement using the web as a platform. I pledge to support the following principles of open government:

Transparency:

Open governments ensure accountability and informed civic participation by conducting their operations and making decisions more openly, and publishing their information for the public to see.

Participation:

Open governments leverage technology to make public information accessible and create innovative tools to engage citizens in government decision-making. Open governments promote collaboration by encouraging city departments to share information and engage citizens.

Accessibility:

Open governments not only make sure public information is shared, but that anyone can find, access and use it.

Efficiency:

Open governments enable programmers, journalists, non-profits, and community activists to develop valuable civic tools powered by public data which help government better serve the public.

•Contact City Leadership

Educating our city officials on civic issues can be the difference between a problem being ignored and acted upon. If we don't tell our representative what we want, or explain what Open Government means, how are they to know or fully understand? Speak up.

Send a Letter to your City Representative

  1. Visit your city's website
  2. Find your representatives under the section commonly labeled City Council or Government. Your mayor will also likely have his or her own section.
  3. To Note: Your city might have a different names for the its main legislative body. Some other terms to look for include Board of Selectmen or Board of Supervisors.
  4. Each city representative will have the option to send them a letter/request either by post or email. Gather all of the relevant email addresses.
  5. Email your city officials. You can use this draft letterwe made as a start. Since you've gathered the email address of your representatives, you can even send your emails directly from the link!

Set-up a Meeting with Your Local Representative

  1. Visit your city council website
  2. Find your city councilor(s)' email under the section commonly labeled city councilor or government.
  3. Request a time to meet via email (either draft your own or refer to the sample below)
    • State the reasons why you are contacting them.
    • State your objective of the meeting
    • Be sure to mention that you are a constituent of the legislator.
    • If applicable, refer the legislator to the relevant bill and let him or her know the specific bill number.
    • Now, here's what we'd say:

    Dear Councilor [Last name],

    I am a constituent in your district representing a group of residents dedicated to open government. Open government is part of a global initiative to use the web as a platform to solve civic problems, create new opportunities for civic engagement and build trust between city leaders and city dwellers. Open government can help our [City name] work better for citizens by [Insert relevant example].

    We would like to arrange a time to meet in your office to discuss open government. Constituents are asking you to take a leadership role in making government more open. Could we arrange a time to meet in your office on [insert date]?

    Linked to this email is a pledge you can take to show your commitment to open government.

    Thanks for your time.

    Sincerely,

    [First, Last name, District]

  4. Follow-up on your email. Call your city councilor's office confirming a time to meet.

    5. Tip: Call way in advance to the date you want!

  5. At the meeting use the slideshow deck (link) and give your city councilmember or leader the open government handout (link).
  6. Ask your city official to sign the Open Impact Pledge (link).

•Speak to City Leaders

City council or committee meetings often allow constituents to provide feedback to government officials - the perfect chance to educate your elected officials on the value of open government.

Request to Speak at a City Meeting

  1. Visit your city's official website and look at their weekly meeting calender for when and where the next city council meeting takes place
  2. Look at the meeting agenda and familiarize yourself with its structure
  3. Consult your city's website to get informed on the details to participating in a city council meeting or hearing.
  4. Fill out a city council meeting or hearing request form online before the submission deadline.

Important to Remember:

  • Citizen speeches on topics not on the agenda are reserved for the public comment portion of the meeting.
  • If there is an agenda item regarding open government, you will be allotted time to speak on that issue before discussion begins.
  • Allotted time can range from 1-5 minutes.
  • Deadlines to submit speaking requests vary

Prepping For Your Speech

  • Time to brainstorm and write. Here's a sample speech if you need some inspiration.
  • A good speech is one that combines well researched facts with effective storytelling.
  • Be direct and concise.
  • Start by explaining open government and its benefit to citizens and government using strong examples from other cities.
  • Emphasize the benefits of open government that will most appeal to your city officials. For example, if your city is facing budget cuts, make sure to talk about the fiscal benefits of increasing the efficiency of government services through technology.
  • End your speech with steps for direct action (refer to pledge/Open Government Resolution)

Tips:

  • Fill city hall with your supporters. The more people you arrive with, the bigger the statement to your elected officials.
  • Make sure to look over the rules and regulations of what you can and can't do while attending city hall. This includes everything from attire (e.g. no logos) and overall decorum (e.g. no applause). They can be strict!
  • Wait to be called up for comments.

Say Your Piece (But keep it to 1-5 minutes)

  • Go up to the podium confidently and thank the person who introduced you by their title and last name.
  • Introduce yourself, starting with your first name, last name and organization (if applicable).
  • If you are arriving with a group of people, let them know you're acting as the spokesperson for that larger group of X people.
  • Thank your audience.

Hackathons

Hackathons are a great way to promote collaboration and discuss solutions to civic issues. They're also a lot fun! They can take place over a weekend or maybe even a few weekends.

Invite friends, allied organizations and government officials to your event and have them hack away at a civic issue. Successful hackathons are those who strive to bring together a diverse field of specialties and interests from designers and developers to government officials and social advocates.

You can hack away at code (i.e make an app or api) or a policy (i.e. drafting an open data policy). Follow these steps and launch your own hackathon.

  1. Secure a Venue - Adequate table space, chairs, and internet access are vital.
  2. Define the Scope - Identify a compelling local problem or need (i.e. public transit, crime mapping, finding city real estate etc).
  3. Partner: Invite speakers to the event and partner with community organizations involved in the field you'll be hacking on. They will provide useful knowledge to the hackers on the community need and have a strong network to bring to the event.
  4. Create an agenda: A lot of time will be spent hacking away at the problem definition, but it is good to create time to break, eat and discuss progress on each project or maybe even hand out prizes at the end! Check out creative currency's schedule. Our friends at Mozilla also has created a very useful schedule guide.
  5. Promotion - Start pushing the event through all available social media channels, i.e. twitter and facebook. Ask your city and community contacts for relevant email lists that you can use to publicize. Post the event on eventbrite (link), facebook, meetup (link) and your local newspaper.
  6. Day of the event - Remember the following:
    • Name badges, sticky notes, pens, registration sheets. Extra points always go to those that can come up with creative low cost prizes.
    • Hackers need fuel - Have food and beverages as participants hack away

City Camp

CityCamp is an unconference focused on innovation for municipal governments and community organizations. They have become widely popular as a way to bring different stakeholders together (i.e. designers, developers and city leaders) to discuss new ways of solving civic problems using the web as a platform. Unconferences are not your typical conference, they are interactive, engaging and the participants play a huge role in directing the conference. Here is a breakdown of how to get started.

Lightning Talks

These talks are to-the-point presentations given at a conference or similar forum. Unlike other presentations, lightning talks last only a few minutes. This event method is great due to its short and sweet style that can effectively communicate a message to its audience. It may be a challenge to make a point in only a few minutes, but when done well a few minutes is all you need to catch people's interest.

Speaker Panel

Bring together a panel (3-4 people ) to talk about open government. A panel opens with the chair introducing the panelists and the topic. Some panels are highly structured with a set time for each panelist to speak. Others employ a more discussion-oriented format with the panelists responding to each other and to audience inquiries throughout the session.

Suggested Event Topics

When running your first event, it helps to have a few predetermined topics or themes to guide the direction of the event. It can also be beneficial to open the floor for suggestions or topics of interest. Topics can include broad, big-picture ideas or can have a particular focus. Here are some ideas:

  • What is open government?
  • Discuss how an open government benefit its citizens
  • Discuss ways open government can improve daily life
  • How can we use open government to increase civic engagement?
  • How does open government solve inefficiencies in city government?
  • Which city datasets should be prioritized for liberation?
  • Critique of existing government websites/portals
