Transparency:

•Facilitate an Open Culture

Interact Through Social Media

Cities across the country are using social media tools to interact with their community -- and citizens are responding with enthusiasm. Why? It’s fast, it’s viral, it can spark civic engagement, it’s widely accessible, and it’s free. Help break down the barriers between city hall and city dwellers. What better way to give constituents the information they want -- and gain the feedback you need?

Hold a Twitter "Townhall”

The city of Palo Alto used Twitter to host Q&A sessions featuring Mayor Yiaway Yeh and City Manager James Keenewith.

Hangout on Google+

President Obama answered questions in a Google+ Hangout immediately after his 2012 State of the Union address to get feedback from citizens on his agenda.

Use Facebook to Solicit Constituent Input

Mayor Emanuel of Chicago hosted the first #AskChicago Facebook Town Hall, making him the first in-office mayor of a major city to hold a live online forum in which questions and concerns were submitted and voted on by the public.

Conduct a Contest on Pinterest

Maryland governor Martin O’Malley used Pinterest to connect with Maryland entrepreneurs by inviting constituents to submit 10 images to sell their idea for a business. Existing small businesses were also eligible to compete.

Live Stream City Meetings

Chicago City Council meetings have been streamed over the Internet since 2007. Ustream, one of the most popular streaming providers, is already used by several cities including Panama City, Hoboken, and Portland to broadcast and archive meetings.

Use Wikis and Apps to Solicit Feedback

Constituent feedback provides vital insights for your city. But busy citizens often don’t have the time to attend public forums; traditional feedback techniques gather opinions that don’t represent the whole community. Technology changes this reality. Check out these three engagement tools you can use in your city:

Use Shareabouts’ Map Tool for Zoning Feedback

New York’s Department of Technology used Shareabouts, a simple mapping tool for gathering input online, to aggregate suggestions for locations of bike share stations. Utilizing this open source tool, constituent recommendations can be collected and referenced during decision making to come to a decision in a much more democratic way.

Create a Wiki for Input on Policies

New York’s Department of Information Technology and Telecommunications used a wiki to collaborate with the public on its policy for open data tech standards and guidelines. The wiki allowed the public to see the proposed policy and offer edits and tips. It was used in tandem with a policy unconference to ensure the city got citizen feedback before adopting the standards and guidelines.

Use Textizen for a Feedback Campaign

The city of Philadelphia is using Textizen to run feedback campaigns. Textizen asks questions on posters in public places and collects citizen feedback via text message. Philadelphia can use the feedback to make more data-driven decisions.

Host a Hack Event

Hack events bring together citizens to craft solutions to civic issues. From designers and developers to government officials and social advocates, participants can “hack away” and use technology to improve government.

Civic App Hackathon

Hackathons promote collaboration and help develop technical apps and solutions to civic challenges. These events pump fresh ideas and innovation into government, help resource-constrained cities address challenges, and connect cities with designers, developers, and the generally curious. Get started planning your hackathon with our tips on how to host a hackathon, or learn from previously held events in Joplin, Tampa, or Austin.

Policy Hack

When drafting policy or creating standards for recently passed legislation, host an event where community members and government officials can come together to discuss and collaboratively improve draft policy. New York Cityhosted the NYC Open Data Policy Hack Day to get feedback when creating technical standards for its open data law.

•Open Data

The Benefits of Open Data

Governments collect data on taxes, health services, education, and much more. While government officials and employees have access to this information, the general public often does not. Opening this data to public use by publishing it online in a standardized format brings benefits to public servants and constituents alike. Benefits such as:

  • Government departments avoid wasting time and resources handling requests for information on an individual basis.
  • Opening data allows other city departments to make use of previously unshared information -- and creates new opportunities for collaboration.
  • Open data improves government transparency and increases public trust; a survey by the open data company Socrata found that 56% of citizens report that they would trust their government moreif they put the majority of government data online.
  • Civic hackers, journalists, non-profits, and even businesses can use the data to build websites and applications for public use, such as: The Landlord Watchlist(New York), SweepAround.us(Chicago) Neighborland(National).
Don't forget to spread the word about the benefits of open data and open government to your colleagues. To get the conversation started, the Open Impact campaign created a handout that you can share with fellow public servants. Download it.

Liberate Department Data

Opening data takes four basic steps:

  1. Select the data you want to release
  2. Apply an open license
  3. Making the data available
  4. Publicize it
The Open Knowledge Foundation has created a comprehensive guideto the process -- and it’s in plain English so that anyone (techie or not) can understand.

Create an Open Data Portal

If you’re the head of a department, or can get your department leader onboard, consider making an open data portal for your department (or, even better, your city!). An open data portal brings all the datasets for a department together in one centralized place that is easy to find accessible by all. This makes it easy for constituents, developers, and other departments to search for the information they need. Data portals are already in use across the United States by 34 states and 15 cities from large (New York: nycopendata.socrata.com) to small (Arvada, Colo.: arvada.org/opendata). See the full list at data.gov/statedatasites.When you’re ready to create your open data portal, follow our six tips for creating an open data portal.

•Improve Your Procurement Process

City governments across the United States spend billions of dollars each year procuring products and services for their cities. Systems for attracting vendors and accepting proposals, however, are often confusing, cumbersome, and slow. This can deter new vendors and companies from competing for government business, which causes the city and its citizens to lose out.

To ensure those with the best-fit solutions vie for a city’s business, we’ve identified six ways you can leverage technology to improve your procurement process:

Create a centralized website listing all RFP, RFQ, RFI opportunities CHALLENGE:When procurement requests are dispersed across departments and websites, it is difficult for new vendors to find out what opportunities are available. ACTION:Centralize RFP, RFQ, and RFIs on a website that is easy to find and linked through the city website’s homepage. The website should allow the user the ability to search opportunities by keyword, as well as sort by relevant category. WHY:By creating a central, easy-to-navigate website where vendors can find information, you’ll help those who want and need information, find it.

Provide data feed or well-formed HTML versions of RFP, RFQ, and RFI opportunities CHALLENGE:When officials scan RFP, RFQ, or RFIs and upload them as PDFs, it's almost impossible for developers to work with the information. ACTION:Provide proposals in well-formed HTML or create a data feed listing all RFP, RFQ, and RFI opportunities. WHY:By making it easier for developers to work with RFP, RFQ, and RFI data, you open the door for the creation of apps and software platforms that can improve the procurement process for the city or the vendors.

Create an online portal for vendors to submit RFP, RFQ, and RFI materials CHALLENGE:To submit a RFP, RFQ, or RFI, a vendor typically has to complete multiple steps and fill out numerous, lengthy forms. ACTION:Create a vendor portal to provide a centralized hub for all completed submission documents. WHY:Allowing vendors to submit materials online cuts down on the time the city spends processing proposals. This frees up your department to spend more time on other things, like marketing and outreach. The portal also helps the vendor track progress and makes it easier for them to identify missing materials.

Create a content management system to track and review submissions CHALLENGE:Multiple parties must review RFP, RFQ, and RFI submissions. When submissions aren’t centralized or consistently tracked, it can be difficult to understand what stage each submission is in the review process. This can significantly increase the time it takes to decide on a vendor. ACTION:Create a content management system to track and review submissions. WHY:Having a content management system to help you track proposals allows you to streamline the review process. This allows you to more quickly come to a decision on a vendor.

Use technology and social media to get the word out to bidders CHALLENGE:It can be difficult for vendors who haven’t sold services and products to the city before to identify when opportunities they are qualified for become available. ACTION:Use social media, online forums, and targeted internet ads to get the word out to interested parties about active requests. For example, use social media like Facebook and Twitter to blast out the word when the request is active. Use YouTube or Vimeo to make a video that describes the request and what an ideal product or service would offer. WHY:By using a variety of channels to advertise, you can reach a broader audience. This can diversify and increase the products and services to choose from.

Develop an intranet with template proposals and a catalogue of past proposals CHALLENGE:Drafting RFP, RFQ, and RFIs requires a lot of resources. Employees writing requests could benefit from up-to-date boilerplate templates, as well as from access to similar past requests. ACTION:Create a central intranet for RFP, RFQ, RFI templates and update regularly as procurement policies change. Include a catalogue of past proposals. WHY:Employees writing request documents should be armed with an up-to-date template that gives them a starting point to create a proposal, as well as access to similar proposals written in the past. This will allow them to focus on defining the nuances of each request to make sure it elicits the most effective responses.

•Implement Open Government Policies

Open government policies lay the framework for building a culture of innovation and openness from the ground up.

The Benefits of Open Government Policies

They catalyze civic innovation and increase transparencyOpen data policies give civic hackers reassurance that they can rely on city data and allow them to build service organizations, businesses, and product models around this data. Open data policies also provide transparency and give citizens, city departments, and city officials equal access to data.

They offer economic and security benefitsOpen source solutions can save cities money on licensing and redistribution costs. Open source solutions can also offer a greater level of security to cities because they are patrolled by a variety of users. The Department of Defense, Google, Facebook, and the White House (to name a few) all have open source policies.

They ensure greater interoperability, and the ability of systems to work togetherWhen you can plug in new software or systems to existing ones, it cuts down on costs, inefficiency, and headaches!

Sample Policies

Use these templates and guides to get started drafting open policies for your city. The open government directive is a good place to begin. Open government policies include:

Open Data and Open Standards LanguageThe open data policy requires departments to publish their data in machine-readable formats and make it available to the public-at-large through a single web portal. It also requires the city to develop open data technical standards, a compliance plan detailing qualifying data, and a timeline to make the data publicly available. This policy also outlines open standards regulations for data. Public Records ActThe Public Records Act requires city agencies to publish their records publicly online through a single web portal maintained by the city. Open Source Procurement PolicyThe open source procurement policy requires agencies to consider open source software when acquiring software. It also requires them to catalogue and share software source code that facilitates the code’s reuse and meets set definitions.

•Establish an Office of New Urban Mechanics

The Mayor’s Office of New Urban Mechanics — initially conceived in Boston and now being replicated in cities across the country — is an organizational model that helps cities innovate by aggregating risk, building partnerships, and encouraging experimentation in government. Projects have ranged from crowdsourcing development of sensor-driven apps to government-to-government resource sharing. This process for civic innovation is one that can adapted by any city. By creating an Office of New Urban Mechanics in your city you can institutionalize a commitment to innovation. Learn more about starting an office of New Urban Mechanics program in your city.
